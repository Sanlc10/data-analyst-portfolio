Statistical Testing · Telecom · α = 0.01

Two prepaid plans, one statistically louder.

Megaline, a prepaid telecom operator, needed to decide which of two plans — Surf or Ultimate — generates more revenue per user. A clean two-sample hypothesis test, plus a regional twist that nobody asked for.

Dataset
500 users · 5 tables · 2018
Tools
Domain
Telecom · Pricing
Year
2025
Business context

A pricing decision dressed as a statistics question.

Megaline's commercial team had a question that sounded simple: where should we steer customers? Surf or Ultimate? The plans differ in pricing, included minutes, messages and data allowances — but customer behaviour rarely matches plan design, so revenue per user is the only honest comparison.

I worked with 2018 data on 500 subscribers across five tables — users, plans, calls, messages and internet sessions. The analysis had to land in a decision the commercial team could defend to marketing finance.

Methodology

Five tables, one decision.

Data integration

Joined and harmonised five datasets. Resolved date types and a handful of edge-case anomalies before aggregating.

Revenue modelling

Computed monthly revenue per user from raw consumption + tariff + overage logic. This was the metric everything else hinged on.

Behavioural EDA

Compared distributions of minutes, messages and MB across the two plans using box plots and summary statistics.

Hypothesis tests

Two-sample t-tests at α = 0.01 — one for plan-level revenue, one for regional revenue (NY-NJ vs everywhere else).

Recommendation

Translated the test outcomes into a budget-allocation and pricing-positioning recommendation.

Python · T-test megaline.ipynb 
from scipy import stats

surf     = total_per_user.query('plan_name == "Surf"')['total_income']
ultimate = total_per_user.query('plan_name == "Ultimate"')['total_income']

alpha  = 0.01
result = stats.ttest_ind(surf, ultimate, equal_var=False)

print(f"t = {result.statistic:.3f}, p = {result.pvalue:.4f}")
if result.pvalue < alpha:
    print("Reject H0 — revenue differs between Surf and Ultimate.")

Two-sample t-test at α = 0.01 between Surf and Ultimate monthly revenue. Equal variances were not assumed — Welch's correction was used.

mask_ny = total_per_user['city'].str.contains('NY-NJ', na=False)

ny_nj_income    = total_per_user[mask_ny]['total_income']

stats.ttest_ind(ny_nj_income, other_income, equal_var=False)

Region filter for the secondary test. Splitting NY-NJ subscribers from the rest let us evaluate whether geography moves revenue independently of plan.

The full notebook walks through monthly distributions for minutes, messages and data — each pair of distributions overlaps almost perfectly, which made the revenue difference all the more striking. The Welch t-test was preferred over Student's t because variances differed across plans (overage events introduce heavy right tails on Surf revenue).


A secondary segmentation by city revealed NY-NJ as a clear outlier in average revenue — likely driven by usage patterns specific to that metro area. The recommendation document for the commercial team included a sketch of a NY-NJ-specific test campaign.

Python · Revenue functionmegaline.ipynb
def monthly_revenue(row, tariff):
    base = tariff['monthly_pay']
    overage_min  = max(0, row['minutes']   - tariff['minutes_included'])  * tariff['min_overage']
    overage_msg  = max(0, row['messages']  - tariff['messages_included']) * tariff['msg_overage']
    overage_data = max(0, row['mb_used']   - tariff['mb_per_month'])      * tariff['mb_overage']
    return base + overage_min + overage_msg + overage_data

The revenue function — codifies plan economics into one defensible computation that becomes the response variable for every downstream test.

Final takeaway
Recommendation

Marketing dollars should chase Ultimate acquisitions — the plan generates significantly higher revenue per user, even though behaviour barely differs. Layer a NY-NJ-specific positioning on top, since that market behaves like its own segment.
