What actually predicts a hit video game.

For Ice, a global video game retailer, I worked through 16,715 titles to understand what drives commercial success. The answer turned out to be less about reviews and more about platform timing and regional fit.

Dataset
16,715 games
Tools

Business context

Planning a 2017 campaign with data through 2016.

Ice is a fictional online retailer with global reach across North America, Europe and Japan. Marketing wanted to plan a 2017 ad campaign and asked a deceptively simple question: which games, on which platforms, in which regions, are worth promoting?

The dataset spanned decades of releases, so the work started with a hard editorial decision — which years matter for a forward-looking campaign? After charting release volume over time, I anchored the analysis on the most recent five-year window where platform mixes still resembled the current landscape.

Filtered the dataset to the modern era and converted the messy user-score column into numerics before testing.

Python · T-test video_games.ipynb 
from scipy import stats

result = stats.ttest_ind(xone_user_score, pc_user_score, equal_var=False)
alpha  = 0.05

print(f"t = {result.statistic:.3f}, p = {result.pvalue:.4f}")
if result.pvalue < alpha:
    print("Reject H0 — user scores differ between Xbox One and PC.")
else:
    print("Fail to reject H0 — no significant difference.")

Two-sample t-test for platform user scores. The test answered: are Xbox One and PC scores drawn from the same population?

Final takeaway
Recommendation

For 2017, the campaign should overweight PS4 / Xbox One in NA and EU while running a separate Nintendo-focused track for Japan. Marketing should not anchor creative on user reviews — they don't predict sales, and the budget should chase platform reach and critical reception instead.
