Marketing Analytics · Cohort Analysis

How we found the channels worth spending on.

An end-to-end audit of the user funnel and marketing spend for Showz, an online events ticketing platform. Goal: pinpoint which acquisition channels actually pay back, and which are quietly draining the budget.

Tools

Marketing Analytics

Business context

A ticketing platform with a marketing problem.

Showz operates an online events ticketing marketplace. Like most early-growth platforms, they had a clear top of funnel but blurry attribution downstream: marketing was spending across multiple acquisition channels, but no one could confidently answer which channels were generating profitable customers — and which were just buying traffic.

The brief was deliberately open: build the lens through which Showz can evaluate marketing performance, end to end. That meant going from raw event logs all the way to a defensible recommendation on where the next marketing dollar should go.

Methodology

A funnel-to-finance pipeline.

Data cleaning

Standardised three datasets — visits, orders and ad costs. Normalised datetime fields and added month/week derived features.

Engagement metrics

Built DAU, WAU and MAU. Layered session length and conversion windows on top.

Cohort analysis

Cohorted users by acquisition month. Tracked time-to-first-purchase and retention curves per cohort.

Unit economics

Computed LTV, CAC and ROMI per acquisition source. Cross-referenced with channel cost data.

Recommendation

Ranked channels by LTV : CAC ratio and translated findings into a budget reallocation proposal.

Final takeaway
Recommendation

If I were sitting on Showz's marketing team, I'd reallocate roughly 30% of the channel-3 budget toward channels 1 and 2 — and revisit channel 3's targeting before any further investment. The cohort data suggests the audience quality, not the volume, is the issue.
